Getting Started

Larger tanks are actually easier for beginners because water parameters are more stable. A 20-gallon tank is ideal for starting out - it's big enough to be forgiving but small enough to be affordable.

Avoid tanks under 10 gallons unless you're experienced, as small volumes are harder to maintain. The "bigger is better" rule exists because more water dilutes waste and temperature swings happen more slowly.

Beginner Essential

Essential equipment for a freshwater aquarium:

  • Tank - Glass or acrylic, 20 gallons recommended for beginners
  • Filter - Sized for your tank (hang-on-back filters are easiest)
  • Heater - If keeping tropical fish (5 watts per gallon rule)
  • Thermometer - To monitor water temperature
  • Water conditioner - To remove chlorine from tap water
  • Test kit - API Master Test Kit is the gold standard
  • Substrate - Gravel or sand for the bottom
  • Light - Basic LED hood light is fine for fish-only tanks

The nitrogen cycle is the biological process that converts toxic fish waste (ammonia) into less harmful substances. It's the most important concept in fishkeeping.

How it works:

  1. Fish produce ammonia (toxic)
  2. Beneficial bacteria convert ammonia to nitrite (also toxic)
  3. Other bacteria convert nitrite to nitrate (less toxic)
  4. You remove nitrates through water changes

To cycle your tank:

  1. Set up your tank with filter running
  2. Add an ammonia source (fish food or pure ammonia)
  3. Test water daily with a test kit
  4. Wait 4-8 weeks until ammonia and nitrite read 0 and nitrates are present

Never add fish until the cycle is complete! Learn more in our glossary.

Essential Beginner

You should wait until your tank has completed the nitrogen cycle - typically 4-8 weeks. The exact time depends on your method:

  • Fishless cycling: 4-8 weeks (recommended)
  • With bottled bacteria: 2-4 weeks (can speed things up)
  • Using established filter media: 1-2 weeks (fastest method)

Your tank is ready when: ammonia = 0, nitrite = 0, and nitrates are present. Don't trust "instant cycle" products completely - always verify with test kits.

Fish Compatibility

The old "one inch per gallon" rule is outdated and often leads to overstocking. Proper stocking depends on:

  • Adult fish size - Not purchase size! A 2" juvenile Oscar will grow to 12"
  • Filtration capacity - Better filtration allows slightly more fish
  • Fish activity level - Active swimmers need more space than sedentary fish
  • Territorial needs - Some fish claim territory regardless of tank size

Use our fish comparison tool to check compatibility and research each species' space requirements. When in doubt, understock - your fish will be healthier and maintenance easier.

Stocking Important

Successful community tanks match fish by:

  • Temperament - Don't mix aggressive with peaceful species
  • Size - Avoid fish that can eat tankmates
  • Water parameters - Similar pH and temperature needs
  • Swimming level - Mix top, middle, and bottom dwellers

Popular peaceful community fish: Tetras, Corydoras catfish, Rasboras, Guppies, Platies, and smaller Gouramis.

Fish to research carefully: Cichlids (many are aggressive), Bettas (males fight), and anything labeled "semi-aggressive."

Water & Chemistry

These are the key water quality parameters every fishkeeper must understand:

Parameter Target Notes
Ammonia 0 ppm Toxic at any level - fish waste product
Nitrite 0 ppm Toxic - created from ammonia
Nitrate <40 ppm Less toxic - removed via water changes

Test weekly with a liquid test kit (not strips). If ammonia or nitrite ever show above 0 in a cycled tank, do an immediate water change.

Water Quality Essential

General ranges for most tropical freshwater fish:

  • Temperature: 74-80°F (23-27°C)
  • pH: 6.5-7.5 (most fish adapt to a range)
  • Ammonia: 0 ppm (always)
  • Nitrite: 0 ppm (always)
  • Nitrate: Under 40 ppm (under 20 ppm is better)
  • GH (hardness): 4-12 dGH for most species

Important: Stable parameters matter more than "perfect" numbers. A fish adapted to pH 7.8 will do better staying at 7.8 than being subjected to constant adjustments trying to reach 7.0.

Parameters Reference

The honest answer: Usually, don't. Most fish adapt to a wide pH range, and chasing a "perfect" number causes more harm than good.

If you must adjust pH:

To lower pH (make more acidic):

  • Add driftwood or Indian almond leaves (slow, natural)
  • Use RO (reverse osmosis) water mixed with tap water
  • Add peat moss to your filter

To raise pH (make more alkaline):

  • Add crushed coral to substrate or filter
  • Use limestone or Texas holey rock
  • Increase aeration (drives off CO2)

Avoid: Chemical pH adjusters - they cause dangerous swings and require constant dosing.

pH Advanced

Tap water requires treatment before use. Municipal water contains chlorine or chloramine that will harm fish.

Always use a water conditioner (like Seachem Prime or API Tap Water Conditioner) that neutralizes:

  • Chlorine
  • Chloramine
  • Heavy metals

Know your tap water: Get a baseline reading of your tap water's pH, GH (hardness), and KH. This helps you choose fish suited to your local water rather than constantly fighting chemistry.

Well water users: Test for heavy metals, as well water can contain iron, copper, or other elements harmful to fish.

Water Quality Beginner

Use a liquid test kit (not strips). The API Freshwater Master Test Kit is the standard recommendation - it tests ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH.

Testing frequency:

  • New tank/cycling: Daily
  • Established tank: Weekly
  • After adding fish: Every 2-3 days for 2 weeks
  • When fish act sick: Immediately

Why liquid over strips? Strips are less accurate, expire faster, and cost more per test over time. The liquid kit pays for itself quickly.

Testing Essential

GH (General Hardness) measures dissolved minerals, mainly calcium and magnesium. It affects:

  • Fish health (they need minerals for bones/shells)
  • Breeding success (some fish need specific ranges)
  • Plant growth (plants use these minerals)

KH (Carbonate Hardness) measures carbonates and bicarbonates. It affects:

  • pH stability (KH buffers against pH swings)
  • Prevents "pH crash" (dangerous sudden drops)

General guidelines:

  • Soft water fish (tetras, discus): GH 3-8, KH 1-4
  • Hard water fish (livebearers, African cichlids): GH 12-20+, KH 10+
  • Most community fish: GH 6-12, KH 4-8

Fish Health

Ich (Ichthyophthirius) appears as white salt-like spots on fish. It's common but treatable if caught early.

Treatment steps:

  1. Raise temperature gradually to 86°F (30°C) over 24-48 hours. This speeds up the parasite's lifecycle.
  2. Add aquarium salt (1 tablespoon per 5 gallons) if your fish tolerate it. Avoid salt with scaleless fish or plants.
  3. Increase aeration - warmer water holds less oxygen
  4. For severe cases: Use ich medication (follow package directions exactly)
  5. Continue treatment for 3-4 days after spots disappear to kill remaining parasites

Prevention: Quarantine new fish for 2-4 weeks before adding to main tank.

Disease Treatment

Fish gasping at the surface is an emergency sign. Common causes:

1. Low oxygen (most common)

  • Increase surface agitation immediately
  • Add an airstone or point filter output at surface
  • Check if tank is overstocked

2. Ammonia or nitrite poisoning

  • Test water immediately
  • If elevated, do 50% water change
  • Add Seachem Prime to detoxify temporarily

3. High temperature

  • Warm water holds less oxygen
  • Check heater, lower temperature if too high

4. Gill disease or parasites

  • Look for red/inflamed gills, excess mucus
  • May need medication
Emergency Symptoms

Quarantine prevents introducing diseases to your main tank. It's the single best thing you can do to protect your fish.

Quarantine tank setup:

  • 10-20 gallon tank (can be bare-bottom)
  • Sponge filter (can seed from main tank)
  • Heater matched to main tank temperature
  • Hiding spots (PVC pipes work well)

Quarantine process:

  1. Add new fish to quarantine tank
  2. Observe for 2-4 weeks minimum
  3. Watch for signs of disease (spots, clamped fins, not eating)
  4. Some keepers do prophylactic treatment (controversial)
  5. Only move healthy fish to main tank

It seems like extra work, but one sick fish can wipe out an entire tank.

Prevention Best Practice

Fish deaths usually have identifiable causes. Common reasons:

New tank syndrome

  • Tank wasn't cycled properly
  • Ammonia or nitrite poisoning

Water quality issues

  • Ammonia/nitrite spikes
  • Extreme pH swings
  • Chlorine from untreated water

Temperature problems

  • Heater malfunction (too hot or cold)
  • Rapid temperature changes during water changes

Disease

  • Introduced by new fish without quarantine
  • Stress-triggered illness

Aggression/Stress

  • Incompatible tankmates
  • Overcrowding

To investigate: Test your water immediately, check temperature, and look for signs of disease on remaining fish.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

A basic fish medicine cabinet for emergencies:

Essential:

  • Seachem Prime - Detoxifies ammonia/nitrite in emergencies
  • Aquarium salt - Treats mild infections, ich, stress
  • Ich medication - (Ich-X, API Super Ick Cure)

Good to have:

  • Methylene blue - Fungal infections, egg treatment
  • General antibiotic - (API E.M. Erythromycin or Maracyn)
  • Anti-parasitic - (API General Cure, PraziPro)

Important: Never medicate without a diagnosis. "Shotgun" medicating with multiple products can harm fish. When in doubt, clean water and observation often work better than random treatments.

Maintenance

Most aquariums benefit from 20-30% weekly water changes. This removes nitrates and replenishes minerals.

Adjust based on your situation:

  • Heavily stocked tanks: 50% weekly
  • Lightly stocked planted tanks: 10-15% every 1-2 weeks
  • Fry/baby fish: 10-20% daily or every other day

Water change tips:

  • Match new water temperature to tank (within 2°F)
  • Always use dechlorinator
  • Gravel vacuum while changing water
  • Never replace all water at once (shocks fish)

Consistency matters more than exact percentages. Regular small changes are better than occasional large ones.

Maintenance Essential

Your beneficial bacteria live mainly in your filter media and substrate. Protect them during cleaning:

Safe cleaning practices:

  • Never clean everything at once - Stagger cleaning tasks
  • Rinse filter media in old tank water - Never tap water (chlorine kills bacteria)
  • Don't deep-clean substrate - Just vacuum the surface
  • Keep filter running - Bacteria die quickly without oxygen flow

What's safe to clean with tap water:

  • Tank glass (outside and inside)
  • Decorations (rinse well before returning)
  • Equipment like heaters, tubing

Avoid: Soap, detergents, or household cleaners - residue is toxic to fish.

Cleaning Important

Clean: Rinse mechanical media (sponges, floss) in old tank water when flow decreases - usually every 2-4 weeks.

Replace: Only when media is literally falling apart. The "replace monthly" advice on cartridge packages is marketing, not fishkeeping.

By media type:

  • Sponges/foam: Rinse monthly, replace yearly or when deteriorating
  • Ceramic rings/bio-balls: Rarely if ever - just rinse occasionally
  • Carbon: Replace monthly if using (many skip carbon entirely)
  • Filter floss: Replace when clogged (cheap, okay to replace often)

Pro tip: If you must replace media, only replace half at a time to preserve bacteria.

Filter Maintenance

Algae is a symptom, not the root problem. Address the cause while cleaning:

Immediate removal:

  • Scrape glass with algae scraper or old credit card
  • Remove heavily affected decorations and scrub
  • Vacuum algae off substrate during water changes

Address the cause:

  • Reduce light - 6-8 hours max, no direct sunlight
  • Don't overfeed - Excess food feeds algae
  • More water changes - Removes excess nutrients
  • Add fast-growing plants - They outcompete algae

Algae eaters help but aren't magic: Nerite snails, otocinclus, and amano shrimp eat some algae but won't fix the underlying imbalance.

Yes, for most tanks. Gravel traps fish waste, uneaten food, and debris that decomposes into harmful compounds.

How often:

  • With each water change (weekly for most tanks)
  • You don't need to vacuum the entire bottom each time - rotate sections

How to vacuum:

  1. Start siphon into bucket
  2. Push gravel vacuum into substrate
  3. Let debris rise up the tube while gravel falls back
  4. Move systematically across the bottom

Exceptions:

  • Planted tanks: Vacuum around plants, not through root zones
  • Sand substrate: Hover above surface, don't dig in
  • Walstad/dirted tanks: Don't disturb the substrate

Feeding

General rule: Feed once or twice daily, only what fish can consume in 2-3 minutes.

Signs of overfeeding:

  • Food sinking to bottom uneaten
  • Cloudy water
  • Ammonia spikes
  • Excess algae growth

Feeding tips:

  • Fish stomachs are roughly the size of their eye
  • It's better to underfeed than overfeed
  • Healthy fish can go 1-2 weeks without food (vacations are fine)
  • Fast one day per week to let digestive systems clear

By fish type:

  • Most community fish: 1-2 times daily
  • Bettas: Once daily or every other day
  • Bottom feeders: Feed sinking food after lights out
Feeding Essential

Variety is key. No single food provides everything fish need.

Quality staple foods:

  • Flakes: Good for surface/mid feeders, but lose nutrients quickly once opened
  • Pellets: More nutrient-dense, less waste, better for larger fish
  • Recommended brands: Hikari, Omega One, New Life Spectrum, Fluval Bug Bites

Supplement with:

  • Frozen foods: Bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia (1-2 times weekly)
  • Vegetables: Blanched zucchini, cucumber, peas (for herbivores)
  • Live foods: Best nutrition but risk parasites

Match food to fish: Carnivores need protein-rich food, herbivores need plant matter, bottom feeders need sinking foods. Research your specific species' diet.

Live Plants

These plants thrive in low light without CO2 or special fertilizers:

Nearly indestructible:

  • Java Fern - Attach to wood/rocks, don't bury roots
  • Anubias - Same as java fern, very slow growing
  • Java Moss - Grows anywhere, great for shrimp
  • Marimo Moss Balls - Just roll them occasionally

Easy stem plants:

  • Water Wisteria - Fast growing, forgiving
  • Hornwort - Can float or plant, grows rapidly
  • Elodea/Anacharis - Hardy, good oxygenator

Easy carpeting alternative:

  • Dwarf Sagittaria - Spreads via runners, no CO2 needed

Start with 2-3 varieties and learn what works in your setup before adding more.

Plants Beginner

You DON'T need CO2 for:

  • Low-light plants (java fern, anubias, crypts)

You MIGHT want CO2 for:

Alternative to injection: Liquid carbon products (like Seachem Excel) provide some carbon without the complexity of CO2 systems, but results are modest.

Substrate choice depends on your goals:

Gravel (easiest):

  • Good for: Fish-only tanks, beginners
  • Pros: Cheap, easy to clean, many colors
  • Cons: Not great for rooted plants without root tabs

Sand:

  • Good for: Bottom dwellers (corydoras, loaches), natural look
  • Pros: Natural appearance, fish can sift through it
  • Cons: Can compact, needs occasional stirring, debris sits on top

Planted tank substrates (Fluval Stratum, ADA Aquasoil):

  • Good for: Serious planted tanks
  • Pros: Nutrients for plants, good for roots
  • Cons: Expensive, can lower pH, needs capping or may cloud water

Tip: You can always add root tabs to inert substrates for plants rather than replacing everything.
